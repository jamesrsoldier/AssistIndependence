Choosing a Medical Alert System

A medical alert system provides peace of mind by ensuring help is just a button press away, whether you are at home or on the go.

Updated February 2026 · 10 min read

In This Guide

  • Why Medical Alert Systems Matter
  • Types of Systems
  • Fall Detection & GPS
  • Monitoring Options
  • Understanding Costs
  • How to Choose
  • Our Recommendations

Medical alert systems have been providing peace of mind to seniors and their families for decades. Originally simple landline-connected devices, today's medical alert systems offer sophisticated features like automatic fall detection, GPS tracking, and smartphone integration. Understanding your options helps you choose a system that fits your lifestyle and provides genuine security.

Why Medical Alert Systems Matter

Falls are the leading cause of injury-related death among adults over 65, and the risk of serious complications increases dramatically when help is delayed. A medical alert system ensures that even if you cannot reach a phone or call for help yourself, assistance can be summoned with the press of a button or automatically through fall detection.

Beyond falls, medical alert systems provide security during cardiac events, strokes, severe allergic reactions, and other emergencies where seconds matter. The monitoring center can dispatch emergency services, contact family members, and provide your medical information to first responders.

For many people, a medical alert system enables continued independent living. Families often worry about elderly relatives living alone, and a reliable alert system can provide reassurance that help is always available. This peace of mind benefits everyone involved.

Types of Medical Alert Systems

Medical alert systems fall into three main categories, each designed for different living situations and activity levels.

In-home systems consist of a base station connected to your phone line or cellular network, plus a wearable help button worn as a pendant or wristband. When you press the button, it communicates with the base station, which connects to the monitoring center. Range is typically 300-1000 feet from the base station, covering your home and immediate yard. These systems are ideal for people who spend most of their time at home and want simple, reliable protection.

Mobile systems are standalone devices that include cellular connectivity and GPS, allowing them to work anywhere with cell coverage. They are larger than simple help buttons, typically the size of a small phone, but still wearable on a lanyard or belt clip. Mobile systems are essential for active seniors who drive, travel, or spend significant time away from home. The GPS feature allows the monitoring center to locate you even if you cannot describe your location.

Smartwatch-style systems look and function like regular smartwatches while including medical alert features. They typically offer fall detection, heart rate monitoring, and GPS tracking in addition to telling time and tracking fitness. These appeal to people who want discreet protection without wearing obvious medical devices. However, they require regular charging and comfort with touchscreen technology.

Fall Detection and GPS Tracking

Fall detection and GPS tracking are two features that significantly enhance the protection a medical alert system provides, though they add to the cost.

Fall detection uses accelerometers and sophisticated algorithms to detect the sudden movements characteristic of a fall: rapid downward acceleration followed by impact and lack of movement. When a fall is detected, the device automatically contacts the monitoring center without requiring you to press a button. This is crucial because many falls result in head injuries or disorientation that prevent the person from manually calling for help.

Fall detection is not perfect. It may miss some falls, particularly slower falls like sliding off a chair. It may also occasionally trigger false alerts from sudden movements like sitting down hard. However, modern systems have improved significantly and catch the majority of serious falls. For anyone at elevated fall risk, the feature is highly recommended.

GPS tracking allows the monitoring center to locate you when you call for help, even if you do not know where you are. This is essential for mobile systems used outside the home. Some systems also allow family members to check your location through a smartphone app, which can provide additional peace of mind.

Advanced systems combine GPS with wifi and cellular tower triangulation for more accurate location indoors, where GPS signals are weak. This matters if you need help in a shopping mall, office building, or someone else's home.

Monitoring Options

All medical alert systems include some form of response when you press the help button or when fall detection triggers. Understanding the monitoring options helps you choose the right level of service.

Professional monitoring connects your device to a 24/7 call center staffed by trained operators. When an alert is received, an operator speaks with you through the device (if you are able to respond), assesses the situation, and dispatches appropriate help. They can call 911, contact your emergency contacts, or simply provide reassurance if it was a false alarm. Professional monitoring typically costs $20-45 per month depending on the system type.

When evaluating professional monitoring, ask about average response times, whether the center is certified (UL-listed is the gold standard), and whether they have access to your medical information and emergency contact list. Also confirm that the center is US-based if that matters to you.

Self-monitoring options are available on some devices, particularly smartwatches. Instead of connecting to a call center, alerts go directly to family members or caregivers via smartphone app or text message. This eliminates monthly fees but requires that your contacts are available and able to respond. Self-monitoring is a reasonable option for lower-risk individuals with engaged family support.

Direct 911 calling is a feature on some devices that bypasses a monitoring center and calls emergency services directly. This can be faster in a clear emergency but eliminates the human assessment that prevents unnecessary ambulance dispatches for false alarms.

Understanding Costs

Medical alert system costs include equipment and monthly monitoring fees, with significant variation based on features and providers.

Equipment costs range from free to several hundred dollars. Many providers offer equipment free with a monitoring contract, though some charge activation fees. Purchasing equipment outright sometimes saves money long-term if you expect to use the service for years. The most basic in-home systems are cheapest, while mobile systems with fall detection cost more.

Monthly monitoring fees for basic in-home systems start around $20-25 per month. Mobile systems with GPS typically cost $30-45 per month. Adding fall detection usually adds $5-10 to the monthly cost. Many providers offer discounts of 10-20% for paying quarterly or annually rather than monthly.

Watch for hidden costs including activation fees, shipping charges, cancellation fees, and equipment return requirements. Some companies offer risk-free trial periods of 30 days, which allows you to test the system before committing.

Medicare generally does not cover medical alert systems, though some Medicare Advantage plans include them as supplemental benefits. Medicaid coverage varies by state. Check with your insurance or contact your local Area Agency on Aging for potential assistance programs.

How to Choose the Right System

Selecting a medical alert system requires matching features to your specific situation and needs.

Consider your lifestyle. If you spend most of your time at home, an in-home system provides reliable protection at the lowest cost. If you drive, garden, visit friends, or travel, a mobile system is essential for protection outside your home. If you are very active and tech-comfortable, a smartwatch option might appeal.

Assess your fall risk. Anyone with a history of falls, balance problems, dizziness, or medications that affect balance should strongly consider fall detection. The additional cost is modest compared to the potential benefit of automatic help when you cannot press the button yourself.

Think about wearing preferences. A device only works if you wear it consistently. Pendant-style buttons work well for some people but feel medical and stigmatizing to others. Wristband options look more like watches. Smartwatch systems are the most discreet. Choose what you will actually wear every day.

Evaluate your support network. Professional monitoring is essential if you live alone without nearby family. If adult children or caregivers check in frequently and can respond quickly, self-monitoring options become viable. Be realistic about how quickly your contacts can actually reach you in an emergency.

Test before committing. Most reputable providers offer 30-day trial periods. Test the device in your daily routine: Does the pendant catch on clothing? Can you hear the base station speaker from your bathroom? Does the mobile device fit in your pocket or purse? Is the button easy to press?
